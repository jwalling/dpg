Introduction

A Disaster Planning Group is forming in November 2023
A building: Kathy R. / C building: Frances B. / D building: John W.
Document resources are saved on Google Drive at https://tinyurl.com/agrc-disaster-plan
To download a document, right click a filename, then select Download from the popout.
A wiki database is in the main folder, titled Disaster-Zim-nn.html (nn=version number)
Download the wiki file and open it in a browser, like FireFox, Chrome, Edge, or Safari.
-- If you are unable to download a file, request it by filename @ Contact

Purpose: This HTML document will be used as a shared wiki-database of online resources for promoting disaster planning at Arrowhead Gardens in Seattle, WA.
I will upload new versions of this document with other documents
to Google Drive https://tinyurl.com/agrc-disaster-plan

This document is created by exporting to a single HTML file from a ZimWiki Notebook, titled Disaster Planning.
The HTML file name is disaster-zim-xx.html where xx is the version number — there will be many versions.
I will Version dates for uploaded versions.

Export instructions: https://www.zim-wiki.org/manual/Help/Export.html

ZimWiki is a free desktop wiki that can run on multiple platforms, including Ubuntu, Windows and MacOS.
It is available for download here: https://www.zim-wiki.org/
Contact me if you would like to have copy of the Disaster Planning Notebook files for use with your ZimWiki program.

Send questions, comments and suggestions to John Walling D424 <ArrowheadResident@gmail.com>

Disaster Planning Stages:

  1. Disaster Preparedness > on going - learn, make plans, and stock at least 2 weeks of survival materials.
  2. Disaster Response > hours to weeks - survive, evacuate or sheltering in place, communicate, and help neighbors.
  3. Disaster Recovery > days to months - return to pre-disaster conditions or move away.

The Seattle Office of Emergency Management hosted a housing recovery seminar in August of 2019. Subject matter experts from Texas shared their experiences with housing recovery in the aftermath of Hurricane Harvey. Christa López is Director of Housing Recovery for the Texas General Land Office and Sarah Labowitz is Assistant Director of Policy and Communications with City of Houston’s Housing and Community Development Department. Christa and Sarah have direct experience in addressing the short and long-term housing recovery activities following the impacts of Hurricane Harvey.
On August 25, 2017, Hurricane Harvey made landfall on the Texas coast, stalled over the Houston area and brought prolonged and widespread flooding. In Houston, over 24,000 families were displaced from their homes and the state of Texas estimates over 1 million homes were impacted. As a presidentially declared disaster, Congress appropriated more than $5 billion to the State of Texas for recovery assistance. The Texas General Land Office estimated the cost of damages from Hurricane Harvey at $160 billion.
Watch the full seminar hear about lessons learned from Hurricane Harvey and how those lessons could be applied in Seattle following a catastrophic earthquake.

My Interpretation of the HIPAA privacy rule:
HIPAA privacy rules do not prohibit people and organizations from asking for vaccine status, if they are not a "Covered Entity"
"Covered entities" cannot provide medical information to entities not covered by HIPAA privacy rules. (Exceptions might be in HIPAA regulations.)
For entities not covered by HIPAA privacy rules, state and local laws may restrict what can be done with vaccine status information.

OEM Emergency Management Trainings

The Office of Emergency Management (OEM) works with Seattle communities to prepare, respond, mitigate impacts, and recover from disasters. As a city, we regularly hear from OEM throughout the summer as they warn us and prepare us for heat waves and wildfire smoke heading toward our region.

OEM also has resources available to the community to prepare for other emergency events. You can go to the department’s Event Calendar to learn more about their virtual training events including Disaster Preparedness: The Basics, where participants gain an overview of the hazards that can impact Seattle and steps you can take to develop a disaster plan, build a disaster supply kit, and organize with neighbors to become better prepared. Disaster Skills Workshops provide training on key skills including water storage and purification and emergency sanitation.
https://www.seattle.gov/emergency-management/about-us/event-calendar/

Your community group, school, or business can also request these trainings for your groups via the OEM Preparedness Training Request Form. OEM also has trainings available for neighborhood groups, workplace preparedness, and emergency planning for childcare providers, all accessible via that request form.
https://forms.office.com/Pages/ResponsePage.aspx

Disaster Skills Workshops covering fire extinguisher use, utility control, water storage and purification, and emergency sanitation are also available in English, Arabic, Amharic, Cambodian-Khmer, Chinese, English, Kiswahili, Laotian, Moldavian, Oromo, Romanian, Spanish, Somali, Tigrinya, Thai, and Vietnamese. You can schedule these trainings via the OEM Training Request Form.
https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSdiVcwZMyNY2zb03jdLksPDrvV-aotApROLSv6ocVe7pUXDog/viewform

Finally, OEM’s website has a plethora of online skills training videos available. You can learn how to shut off your gas or water, and how to use a fire extinguisher at the OEM website. This website also includes links to online courses available from the Federal Emergency Management Association, including Animals in Disasters: Awareness and Preparedness.
https://www.seattle.gov/emergency-management/community-engagement#onlinecourses
